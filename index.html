This Foundations tab covers the concepts that anchor every project (the universal baseline, the brand layer, the assets Creative produces, and the categories that govern hours). The Process tab covers the workflow itself.

Creative Universal Baseline

WCAG AAA accessibility. Every Creative Package meets WCAG AAA compliance. AAA is stricter than the AA standard most institutions commit to, and it reflects iDesign's stand-out commitment to inclusive learning. All color combinations, contrast ratios, alt-text conventions, and design decisions honor this standard.

Brand Foundations

iDesign Creative is an extension of our partners' brand teams. Two Creative teams come together to translate the partner's brand into beautiful course developments inside the LMS. Brand Foundations is the shared brand layer that grounds every graphic the combined team produces: colors, typography, logos, voice, imagery style, and the other elements that make a course look and feel on-brand.

Brand Foundations are established at project start during Program Planning Phase 1, and referenced by Visual Designers every time they make a banner, an icon, or any other course graphic.

Where each brand element comes from

Every brand element falls into one of three buckets based on its source. The buckets are categories, not sequential steps. Many elements get worked simultaneously.

Receive
From the partner's brand team as-is.
  • Brand guidelines (website, PDF, or document)
  • Color palette
  • Typefaces
  • Logo files (SVG preferred)
  • Voice and tone (when documented)
  • Imagery library (when available)
Decide
Jointly interpreted for the LMS context by the two Creative teams.
  • Color compliance (WCAG AAA)
  • Font CSS access decisions
  • Logo usage in LMS placements
  • Imagery selection and treatment
  • Pattern and texture application
  • Iconography style direction
Fill
iDesign Creative creates when the partner doesn't have it.
  • Document templates (Word)
  • Presentation templates (PPT)
  • Illustration style guides
  • Brand patterns and backgrounds
  • Proprietary SVG assets
  • Iconography (when style isn't established)

What needs to exist before work can begin

Brand Foundations have two tiers. Whether each item is received, decided on, or filled, the must-haves must exist in some form before Visual Designers can start producing assets.

Required

Must-haves

Creative work cannot begin until these exist for the partner.

  • Brand guidelines (website, PDF, or document)
  • Color palette and usage rules
  • Typefaces and usage rules
  • All logo files and rules (SVG preferred)
  • Iconography style (separate from icon assets themselves)
Optional

Nice-to-haves

Elevate the package when present. Work proceeds without them.

  • Document and presentation templates
  • Voice and tone
  • Imagery style
  • Illustration style
  • Patterns, backgrounds, icons, or other proprietary SVG assets
Who carries the final brand decisions for the LMS context. The partner's Brand Team holds the brand. iDesign Creative extends that brand into the LMS environment. For decisions specific to how the brand lives inside course graphics (color compliance, type rendering at small sizes, logo placement on banners, accessibility considerations), iDesign Creative carries the call. The two teams stay in sync; iDesign Creative is the closer.

Creative Assets

The Creative Assets layer is what Creative produces and delivers per project. It carries two artifact types.

Graphics

Designed visual assets: banners, avatars, icons, and the rest of the components in the inventory below. These are what learners see in the LMS.

ELT Specifications

Written specifications for the E-Learning Technology team to build CSS/HTML templates when custom CSS access is part of the contract. One set per project: the LMS UX Resource.

The three asset categories

Every Creative Asset falls into one of three categories that determine how it's produced and billed.

Generic   Generic

One design created and delivered, used across all courses as-is. Not a starting point for more.

Hours: one-time creation only
Templated   Templated

Master template created once + course-specific application built from it for each course.

Hours: template + (per-course × N courses)
Ad-hoc   Ad-hoc

Standalone work, often unique per course. Separate scope discussion required.

Hours: case-by-case
Why categorization works this way. Categorization is a hours-control mechanism first. It exists to keep Creative Packages affordable and deliverable inside a Tick budget. Components are categorized as Generic when per-course customization would multiply hours unsustainably. Worked example: if a Module Banner were customized per course, a 25-course SOW with 8 modules each becomes 200 banners. Hundreds of hours consumed for an asset whose function ("a module is starting") never changes. The economics drive the categorization. The cognitive consistency benefits (Mayer, Cognitive Load Theory) follow from it.

Scope-risk flags

Some components carry scope risk and require explicit handling. The recommendation engine flags these clearly to both partner and Creative when they're in scope.

Custom Illustrations scope risk
Historically blow scope. Requires explicit guardrails when included: revision cap, complexity tier, subject-matter rules. Sized as Large (10 hrs), Medium (7.5 hrs), Small (5 hrs), Extra Small (2.5 hrs).
Infographics high scope risk
Historically very high scope risk. Tagged in the recommendation engine. Sized as Large (10 hrs), Medium (8 hrs), Small (4 hrs). Storyboards must be approved before design begins.
Faculty Avatar partner dependency
Partner-dependency item. Requires a high-quality headshot photo before work can begin. Recommendation engine flags the dependency, not just the hour budget.

Hour structure

Every package is calculated as:

Total hours = Program-level setup/admin (one-time)
            + Generic asset creation (one-time per SOW)
            + Templated asset template creation (one-time per SOW)
            + (Course-specific creation × Number of courses)
            + Per-course setup/admin × Number of courses
            + Video Services Support Assets (if in scope: 20 / 45 / (45 + sized Custom Illustrations) hrs by tier)
            + AI video support (if in scope: Ad-hoc, separate scope conversation)
            + Ad-hoc work (case-by-case)
Existing partner adjustment. If the partner has had a previous SOW, skip setup/admin AND generic/template creation. Existing partners use previously created templates for new SOWs. Only per-course graphics and per-course setup/admin are calculated.

Hour estimates per component live in the Creative Calculator and reflect Creative's historical experience. These are the working baseline. Confidence ratings and Tick validation are future additions.

% of scope
Working metric

The metric the team uses to assess whether a package fits its Tick allocation.

% of scope = (Total package hours / Tick hours allotted) × 100
RangeWhat it means
Under 95%Comfortable fit. Room for the unexpected (revisions, late requests, Ad-hoc additions).
95% to 100%At budget. No slack. Track Tick burn closely.
Over 100%Over budget. Either scope needs to come down or hours need to be negotiated.

% of scope surfaces in the Package Explorer below and on every Living Project Page, so the team sees fit immediately, not after the work is done.

Recommendation Engine (The Calculator)

The recommendation engine is the tool that turns dimensions of variation into a Creative Package proposal.

Inputs
  • LMS
  • Hours budget
  • Number of courses in SOW
  • Partner constraints (existing vs. new, template limits, CSS access, etc.)
Outputs

For each partner SOW, the engine produces multiple package options. Each option includes:

  • Components included (LMS-filtered, hours-bounded)
  • Hour breakdown by component
  • Pros and cons for that configuration
  • Learner experience benefits, grounded in research
Behavior

The engine respects:

  • Component support per LMS (Brightspace doesn't have Icons; Homepage is Canvas-only).
  • The Generic / Templated / Ad-hoc cost structure.
  • Existing vs. new partner adjustments (existing partners skip setup/template work).
  • Scope-risk flags (Custom Illustrations require guardrails; Infographics get a high-risk flag).
  • Partner-dependency flags (Faculty Avatar requires headshot pipeline).
Substitution. The engine produces component pairings dynamically as package options based on inputs. There is no fixed canonical set the engine starts from. It assembles options per-project from the inventory available to that LMS, within the hour budget, respecting partner constraints, scope-risk flags, and partner-dependency flags. When a configuration doesn't fit the budget, the engine substitutes components with similar hour costs. UCF's Custom Canvas configuration is the working example: the engine produced a configuration that used 3 Ad-hoc navigation buttons in place of the standard Canvas Homepage to fit 80 hours.
Research grounding. The engine's pairing logic is informed by Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (signaling, spatial contiguity, coherence), Cognitive Load Theory (consistency reduces extraneous load), and visual hierarchy principles. iDesign-specific pairing wisdom from past projects layers on top as the engine learns from outcomes.

North Stars

A North Star is the one-paragraph articulation of what a specific partner's Creative Package is. It's the compass referenced throughout the project. Used in deliverables, shared with partners, kept on hand to keep everyone on the right path.

Audience

North Stars are written to be partner-facing, cross-functional iDesign team-facing, AND Creative-facing. One paragraph that works for all three audiences.

Working Principles

The principles that govern every output, every conversation, and every design decision.

  1. Comparison principle. At every fork between industry-standard practice and current iDesign practice, surface both with pros and cons. GDM decides.
  2. Scaffold-first, anchored to reality. Industry-standard research is grounded in GDM's existing process flowchart and LMS realities.
  3. Voice and culture filter. Every output feels designed for GDM and her team: her voice, suggestions that feel natural, thoughtful about Creative team culture.
  4. Description interview before execution. A Product / Process / Performance Description interview sets the working contract before Task 1.
  5. Lightweight by design. The Companion produces the minimum that delivers value. No bureaucratic overhead.
  6. Integration over replacement. Tick, Asana, Figma, PageProof, and existing project documentation keep their authority.
  7. Asana-first for tracking. All tracking-related work lives in Asana. Never parallel tracking artifacts.
  8. Single primary partner-facing artifact per workflow. One polished output that serves all audiences, not duplicate views.
  9. Leadership-layer surfacing. Team management decisions become visible alongside operational outputs, especially during maternity leave coverage.
  10. Load-lightening test. Every output must lessen GDM's work. If it adds work, it doesn't get built.
  11. Maintain-without-Claude test. Every output works even if the Companion disappears.
  12. Authority. Declaring the canon, the requirements, and the scope is a leadership move and remains GDM's call.
  13. Authority of real-only data. Mockups and visual examples can use placeholder names. Anything operational uses only real partners and real data.

Escalations and FAQ

Prepared responses for the situations and questions that come up around Creative's work. Each entry has a Response, Key facts, Tone, and When this comes up section. Click any entry to expand.

1 Tier 1. Highest priority 10 scenarios
01 Post-approval change request to a previously approved design
Response (GDM's voice)
"This was approved during Program Planning. Any changes at this stage would require reopening that work: a redesign of the affected asset(s), additional PageProof review rounds, documentation updates, and potential schedule impact on courses already in development. We don't have hours left in the current budget to accommodate this. If the change is essential, we'd need an additional bucket of hours in Tick before we can proceed."
Tone

Firm but never defensive. Articulate impact clearly. Don't apologize for the process.

When this comes up

After Program Planning closes. Often weeks into Design and Delivery when SMEs or partner admins surface preferences they didn't raise during the official review process.

02 Scope creep request mid-project
Response (GDM's voice)
"Happy to take a closer look at this. Before we move forward, a few things to know: this is outside the currently approved scope. An hour estimate for the addition would be [X hours] based on our standard hour estimates. Adding it would require additional hours in Tick before work can begin."
Tone

Collaborative tone, firm position. Open the door to conversation without committing the team to absorbing the work.

03 "How much will it cost to add or change [X]?"
Response (GDM's voice)
"Let me put a number to it. Based on what's involved: [list affected work, template redesign, additional review rounds, per-course application × N courses, documentation updates]. The estimate is [X hours]. That would need to be negotiated as additional hours in Tick."
Tone

Matter-of-fact. The team is providing data so others can make budget decisions.

04 Authority confusion. Who from the partner is actually approving Creative work?
Response (GDM's voice)
"Before we move forward with any redesign or change request, I need to confirm who from [partner] has authority over Creative work. There's overlap between [X internal team] and [Y approver] that we should clarify. Otherwise we risk doing work that gets overturned later by another stakeholder."
Tone

Curious and clarifying, not adversarial. Frame as protecting the partner's investment.

05 Partner asks to add Custom Illustrations mid-project
Response (GDM's voice)
"Custom Illustrations are something we offer as add-ons. They need a separate scope conversation. Before we can take this on, we'd need explicit guardrails (revision cap, complexity tier, subject-matter rules), additional hours in Tick, and confirmation that timeline allows for it. Custom Illustrations historically blow scope without these guardrails in place."
Key facts

Sizes: Large (10 hrs), Medium (7.5 hrs), Small (5 hrs), Extra Small (2.5 hrs). Each instance is a separate creation, not templated.

06 Partner asks to add Infographics
Response (GDM's voice)
"Infographics are one of the higher scope-risk components we offer. They consistently exceed estimated hours and often require many revision rounds. Before adding any, we'd need a clear content brief upfront (we don't design without approved storyboards), additional hours in Tick, and a realistic timeline."
Key facts

Sizes: Large (10 hrs), Medium (8 hrs), Small (4 hrs). Tagged "high scope risk."

07 Partner asks to add Video Services mid-project
Response (GDM's voice)
"Video Services is sold separately, and the scope was set at the start of the project. If the partner now wants Video Services added, that goes through Video Services to sell the tier, and we'd need additional hours in Tick to create the supporting graphics that match the tier they choose. Quick math: Tier 1 adds 20 hours, Tier 2 adds 45 hours, Tier 3 adds 45 hours plus the Custom Illustrations work."
Key facts

Video Services is sold by tier. The PBD drives Creative's scope. AI videos have no defined Creative support structure; handled per-project.

08 Rush timeline pressure ("we need to move really fast on this")
Response (GDM's voice)
"I hear you on the urgency. Creative timelines are built around our standard 13-business-day per-course cycle for the Design and Delivery work. Pushing faster means either reducing scope (fewer components per course), or adding hours to compress the workflow."
09 "When can we show the partner what the LMS will look like?"
Response (GDM's voice)
"The partner sees the Creative work in PageProof rounds during Program Planning Weeks 2 and 3. The LMS-built version comes later, once ELT has built the template. Showing too early creates expectations we can't fulfill yet."
10 PM doesn't provide block plan or due dates on time
Response (GDM's voice)
"We need the block plan to set CSG due dates correctly. Without it, the 13-business-day Design and Delivery cycle for each course gets compressed or misaligned. Let's get the block plan in place this week so Visual Designers can plan their work."
2 Tier 2. Common, important 10 scenarios
11 "Can we change [previously approved design]?"
Response (GDM's voice)
"Possibly. But it depends. Anything approved during Program Planning would require reopening that work, which means additional hours in Tick and a budget conversation. The brand authority's approval at Program Planning is meant to lock the visual direction so we can produce at scale during Design and Delivery. If the change is essential, I'll put together an hour estimate to support the budget conversation. If it's a preference rather than a need, we may want to defer until the next SOW."
Tone

Open, not gatekeeping. Frame as protecting the existing work and the partner's budget.

When this comes up

Common during Design and Delivery as new partner-side voices see assets in context and have preferences. Often arrives as casual questions that need careful handling.

12 Another lead on the project pushes for "something to show" too early
Response (GDM's voice)
"I get the desire. Partners want to see something fast. We don't have anything credible to show until Week 2 because the first week is information gathering and brand asset receipt. Showing rough work too early creates the wrong impression of where we are and risks the partner anchoring on something that's not the final direction. What we CAN share in Week 1: the package options we've identified for them, the project's guiding direction, and our overall approach to their LMS. That gives the partner a sense of the shape of things without misrepresenting where we are."
Key facts

Program Planning Phase 1 is intentionally setup; no designs exist. The recommendation engine outputs concrete package options early. The north star paragraph is partner-shareable from Week 1 onward (draft form).

Tone

Educational. Many leads don't understand Creative's process; this is a teaching moment, not a fight.

When this comes up

Almost every Week 1. Among the most frequent escalation patterns.

13 Design Lead overrides Creative decision without consulting
Response (GDM's voice)
"I want to flag this so we can stay aligned. [decision] was made without bringing Creative in, and it conflicts with what we've established with the partner and brand authority. Before this goes further, I want to make sure we're not creating a downstream issue with brand consistency, approved work, or partner expectations. Can we set up a quick call to talk through the change and its implications?"
Key facts

Brand decisions involving Creative scope require Creative's input before final. Design Lead owns the Week 4 partner deliverable but not Creative's scope decisions. Misalignment here creates partner-facing inconsistency.

Tone

Collaborative but assertive. Frame as preventing downstream issues, not as territorial defense.

When this comes up

When project communication breaks down between leads. Often when Design Leads talk to partners or other leads without Creative present.

14 Cross-team coordination blocks Creative timeline
Response (GDM's voice)
"Creative is waiting on [specific input: dates, brand assets, contact information, course metadata] from [team or person] before we can proceed. The current block is causing downstream impact: [specific impact, e.g., ELT can't build templates, Visual Designer can't be assigned, course can't kick off]. I need [specific input] by [date] to keep [course or courses] on schedule. Can you help me get this unblocked?"
Key facts

Creative's work depends on upstream inputs from PM, Design Lead, Lead ID, partner Brand Team. Standard inputs needed at each phase are documented in the requirements model. Delays cascade through the 13-business-day Design and Delivery cycle and into ELT's build window.

Tone

Direct, action-oriented. Name the block, the impact, the deadline, and the ask. Avoid blaming.

When this comes up

Throughout projects. Most often when PMs, Design Leads, or Lead IDs are slow to provide critical inputs.

15 Faculty headshot not ready when Avatar work needs to start
Response (GDM's voice)
"Avatar work depends on receiving a high-quality faculty headshot photo. Without it, Visual Designer can't begin per-course application. Three paths: (1) we hold the work until the headshot arrives and adjust the timeline; (2) we use a placeholder and revise once the headshot lands (this means rework); or (3) we drop the Avatar from this course's package. Let me know which makes the most sense given the constraints."
Key facts

Faculty Avatar is a Templated component requiring partner-supplied headshot. Headshot quality matters. Low-quality photos can't be salvaged without significant rework. This is a partner-dependency flag in the canon.

Tone

Practical and option-focused. Give the asker something to decide rather than just a problem.

When this comes up

Throughout Design and Delivery for any course with Avatar work in scope. Often surfaces in Phase 2.

16 "Why does this take so long?"
Response (GDM's voice)
"Creative work follows a structured cycle for good reasons: 13 business days per course in Design and Delivery (5 design + 5 review + 3 delivery) gives Visual Designers time to do the work, the LID time to review thoroughly, and time for revisions and approvals. The 4-week Program Planning phase ensures the foundation (brand foundations, project direction, package decisions, accessibility commitments) is solid before any per-course work begins. Cutting any of these phases short results in rework, brand inconsistency, or quality issues. All of which cost more in the end than the original timeline."
Key facts

Standard Design and Delivery cycle: 13 business days per course. Program Planning: 4 weeks per SOW. Industry-standard discovery and design timelines align with this structure.

Tone

Educational, calm. This is one to answer once and reference repeatedly.

When this comes up

From partners, project leads, and sometimes internal leadership. Often when a course is behind schedule.

17 "What's the value of [X component]?"
Response (GDM's voice)
"[Component] serves [specific value] for learners. [1-2 sentence learner-experience rationale grounded in research]. Our documentation includes each component's purpose and the research behind it. Specifically for [component]: [reference details]. Want me to walk through how it shows up in the learner experience?"
Key facts

The canon documents each component's purpose, LMS support, and learner-experience value. Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning, Cognitive Load Theory, and visual hierarchy research ground component pairings. Each component connects to a specific learner outcome.

Tone

Confident, informed. The canon and research are the backup.

When this comes up

From project leads questioning scope, from partners during reviews, from internal leadership in budget conversations.

Key facts

Templates are part of Creative's quality system. Editing externally creates risk. ELT's LMS templates depend on Creative's templates staying consistent. Brand foundations require Creative's eye on every change.

Tone

Firm and protective. The "no" is in service of quality, not gatekeeping.

When this comes up

Occasionally from partner admins who want to make small tweaks themselves.

28 "What's the difference between Generic, Templated, and Ad-hoc assets?"
Response (GDM's voice)
"Generic assets are designs created once and used across all courses. Like the Icon Set, Footer, or Module Banners. Templated assets are master templates we create once, then apply per course. Course Banner, Course Card, Homepage, Avatar. Ad-hoc assets are case-by-case work, usually unique per course or per request. Custom Illustrations, Infographics, Badges. The categories determine how each gets produced and how hours are calculated."
Key facts

Category definitions are in the canon. Hours pattern differs by category: Generic = one-time, Templated = template + per-course, Ad-hoc = case-by-case. This shapes the recommendation engine's package options.

Tone

Educational, plain. Avoid jargon when explaining.

When this comes up

From new project leads, internal team members, or anyone trying to understand Creative's structure.

29 "Why aren't you using AI for this?"
Response (GDM's voice)
"Where we ARE using AI: planning, research, drafting content, briefing Visual Designers, generating workspace structures, capturing feedback. Where Visual Designers stay essential: the actual visual craft. Color choices, typography, illustrations, perceptual accessibility checks. iDesign Creative's brand IS the visual craft. AI generates; it doesn't design. Replacing the design step with generative AI would compromise our quality differentiator and our WCAG AAA accessibility commitment, which requires human perceptual review."
Key facts

AI capabilities: text, code, planning, generative SVG. Visual Designer expertise: taste, brand intuition, accessibility judgment, polished output. WCAG AAA requires human perceptual checks that AI can't perform.

Tone

Confident, informed. This is a real question that deserves a real answer.

When this comes up

From leadership (especially Creative Director) and from project leads exploring efficiency.

30 "Why do we need to do all the brand work in Program Planning?"
Response (GDM's voice)
"Program Planning is when we establish the foundation that everything else builds on. Brand foundations, package decisions, project direction, accessibility commitments. Doing this work upfront means each course in Design and Delivery moves fast and consistently because the decisions are already made. Without Program Planning, we'd be making brand decisions per-course, which creates inconsistency and costs more in the long run. The 4-week investment up front saves much more time across the SOW."
Key facts

Program Planning is per-SOW (one-time); Design and Delivery is per-course (repeatable). Brand foundations and package decisions cascade into every course. Skipping or compressing Program Planning creates cascading rework.

Tone

Educational, calm. Reference the structure documented in the requirements model.

When this comes up

From new partners or project leads who don't yet understand the per-SOW versus per-course structure.

Pressure-test methodology

The Companion gets pressure-tested in three ways before it ships to the team, and on a recurring cadence after.

  1. Historical replay. Run UCF and Iona through the canon and recommendation engine as if they were starting today. Compare to what actually happened.
  2. Scenario drills. Five real situations drafted by a team member from the Escalations and FAQ. Timed. Reviewed.
  3. Findability test. Team member finds answers to canon and process questions in the Companion. 90-second threshold flags navigation gaps.

Cadence. Pre-launch (before maternity leave). Quarterly during leave. Annually after.
